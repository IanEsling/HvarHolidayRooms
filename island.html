Hvar has a long and diverse history with archeological remains of Hvar culture going back as far as 3500BC. The Greeks settled in 385BC calling the island Pharos - from which its modern name is derived - and the original document describing the division of the fertile farming land around the town of Stari Grad still exists today and has led to the plain being designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site. The island's location in the middle of the main sea routes of the area has ensured signs and traces of history for over 2000 years have been left in many ways and provide a fascinating background to any visit to Hvar.

The main town of Hvar Town is a bustling, modern port town on the western tip of the island, with glamorous yachts lining the central harbour and the surrounding main square filled with busy restaurants, bars and clubs that stay open till late into the night. There's more to the town than the hectic party atmosphere and floating gin palaces though and exploring the narrow old cobbled alleyways of the old town can be a pleasure in itself.

Hvar is rightly famous for its beautiful sunny climate and the abundance of heather, lavender, rosemary, thyme and sage that grow freely all over the island's hills and groves and provide the wonderful sights and smells that accompany any journey around Hvar, especially the numerous small roads and tracks winding through the countryside. The island is famous for much that grows here, the wine, figs, rosemary oil and lavender products in particular.

The seas around Hvar are warm and crystal clear making it a popular destination for diving and sailing enthusiasts and the hundreds of beautiful beaches lining the coast of the island make it popular with everyone else. The sea has had an obvious influence on the cuisine of the island too, octopus salad and black risotto being two particular specialties of the island, although everywhere from the smallest village kobana to the smartest town centre restaurant offers an amazing variety of fresh seafood.
